## Pre-Session: April Sets the Rules

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Before anyone starts riffing on headlines and hero sections, we need to do the positioning work. A homepage is not a creative exercise. It's the operationalization of your positioning. If your positioning is wrong, no amount of beautiful design will save you. If your positioning is right, even a mediocre homepage will convert.

So we're going to go through my framework. And we're going to fight about it. Then — and only then — do we write a homepage.

Here are the questions we need to answer, in order:

1. **What are the competitive alternatives?** Not competitors — alternatives. What do your best-fit customers do today if SkillGap doesn't exist?
2. **What are your unique capabilities?** What can you do that the alternatives can't?
3. **What value do those capabilities deliver?** Not features — value. What outcome does the customer get?
4. **Who are your best-fit customers?** Not "everyone who wants to learn AI." The specific segment that cares most about your differentiated value.
5. **What market category do you belong to?** This sets buyer expectations and determines what your homepage gets compared to.

Once we have those answers, the homepage writes itself. Let's go.

## Round 1: Competitive Alternatives

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Steffan, if SkillGap didn't exist, what would your best customers do instead? And I mean ACTUALLY do — not what they should do.

> **STEFFAN:** Honestly? Most of them would open YouTube, search "how to use Cursor" or "AI for marketers," watch 3-4 videos of wildly varying quality, maybe try the tool for 10 minutes, get distracted, close the tab, and repeat the cycle in two weeks feeling slightly more anxious. Some would buy a Udemy course and never finish it. A few would sign up for a Maven cohort. The most motivated ones would cobble together their own learning from Twitter threads, blog posts, and Discord communities.

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Good. So let me map the real alternatives:

1. **YouTube + self-directed googling** — Free, infinite content, zero structure, zero accountability, zero proof you learned anything. This is what 80% of your market is doing.
2. **Online courses (Udemy, Coursera, LinkedIn Learning)** — Structured but static, outdated within months, passive watching, no real practice, no community, cheap but low completion rates.
3. **Cohort-based courses (Maven, Reforge, On Deck)** — High-quality but expensive ($500–2,000), one topic at a time, fixed schedule, no ongoing skill tracking.
4. **Doing nothing / figuring it out at work** — The status quo. People muddle through, learn reactively when they hit a wall, never develop systematic fluency.
5. **Corporate L&D programs** — Formal but generic, usually stale curriculum, not AI-specific, compliance-driven not outcome-driven.

**MICHAEL SEIBEL:** I'd argue alternative #1 and #4 are 90% of the market. Most professionals aren't buying Maven courses. They're watching YouTube and winging it. That's who you're actually competing against.

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Exactly. And this is critical for the homepage. Most positioning frameworks make you obsess over named competitors. But your real competition is *inaction and chaos*. The YouTube tab. The anxiety spiral. The "I'll get to it next week." Your homepage needs to position against THAT, not against Coursera.

**JASON FRIED:** This is a huge insight. Don't build a homepage that says "we're better than Udemy." Build one that says "stop drowning in YouTube tabs and actually learn this stuff."

## Round 2: Unique Capabilities

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Now — what can SkillGap do that none of those alternatives can? I want genuine differentiation, not marketing fluff.

**PARKER CONRAD:** The compound system. No alternative has all four of these working together: content intelligence that stays current automatically, task-first learning with real tools, live rooms with other professionals, and a verified skill graph that builds as you learn. YouTube has content but no structure. Maven has structure but no persistence. LinkedIn Learning has breadth but no practice. Nothing else has rooms. And nothing else has the skill graph.

The compound nature IS the differentiation. Each system makes the others better. Paths feed the skill graph. Rooms create accountability for path completion. The skill graph creates sharing that drives growth. You can't get this by combining three other tools.

**ANDREW CHEN:** I'd add: freshness. The intelligence engine means SkillGap has a learning path about a new tool or feature within hours of launch. Courses take months to produce. YouTube creators take weeks. By the time Udemy has a "Claude Code" course, SkillGap has already had 500 professionals complete a path on it. That's a structural advantage that compounds over time.

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Let me push back on the compound system argument. Parker, I know that's your thesis for Rippling, and I think it's right for SkillGap too. But "compound system" is an internal concept. Customers don't buy compound systems. They buy outcomes. What's the OUTCOME of all these systems working together?

**PARKER CONRAD:** Fair. The outcome is: you sit down for 40 minutes, you learn something current and relevant, you build something real with a real tool, you get feedback, you have proof you can do it, and you did it alongside other people so you'll come back tomorrow. And over time, you have a verified portfolio of AI skills that shows your career trajectory. No other product produces that full outcome.

**JASON FRIED:** That's good but it's still a lot of things. What's the ONE thing? If someone reads one sentence about SkillGap and decides whether to click, what's the sentence?

**MICHAEL SEIBEL:** I'll take a crack at it: "Get AI-fluent in 40 minutes a day." No — that's too generic. "Build real AI skills with real tools, alongside real people." Closer. The key differentiators are: real practice (not just watching), real proof (not just a certificate), and real people (not learning alone).

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Good. Let me synthesize the unique capabilities into what I call "differentiated value clusters":

**Cluster 1: You DO the thing, not watch the thing.** Task-first design. Real tools. Real missions. AI feedback on what you built. No other learning platform at this price point makes you practice with the actual tool on a real task and evaluates your work.

**Cluster 2: It's current as of THIS week.** Intelligence engine monitors the landscape. Paths generated from the freshest content. When Claude ships a feature Tuesday, there's a path Wednesday. Courses are 6 months stale by the time you buy them.

**Cluster 3: You're never learning alone.** Live rooms. Sprinting alongside other professionals. AI hosts. The accountability and energy of a co-working space, applied to skill development. No course platform has this.

**Cluster 4: You build proof, not just knowledge.** Verified skill graph. Shareable credentials with evidence. Not "I watched 10 hours of video" but "I demonstrated proficiency in Prompt Engineering across 4 missions." Portable. Yours forever.

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Now — who cares MOST about these four value clusters? Not "all professionals." The tightest possible segment.

**MICHAEL SEIBEL:** The strategy doc says "working professionals who see AI transforming their field and feel the urgency to keep up." That's pretty broad. Can we narrow it?

**APRIL DUNFORD:** We have to. If you try to be for everyone, your homepage speaks to no one. Who's the person who would read those four value clusters and say "shut up and take my money"?

**ANDREW CHEN:** It's the person who's already tried to learn and failed. They've watched the YouTube videos. They've maybe started a Udemy course. They KNOW they need this. But the gap between watching and doing has defeated them. The motivation isn't "I'm curious about AI." It's "my job is changing and I can't keep up and everything I've tried hasn't worked."

**JASON FRIED:** That's the emotional truth. It's not aspiration — it's anxiety. These people aren't excited about AI. They're scared of falling behind. The homepage needs to name that feeling honestly, not dress it up in inspirational language.

**APRIL DUNFORD:** I agree. Let me define the best-fit customer profile:

**Primary:** Mid-career professionals (3-15 years experience) in knowledge work — engineering, marketing, product, design, data, operations — who have ALREADY felt the impact of AI on their role. They've tried to self-teach. They've failed or plateaued. They know they need structured help but they don't have time for a course or the budget for ongoing coaching. They can spend $100/month without manager approval. They're motivated by fear of irrelevance as much as desire for growth.

## Round 4: Market Category

**APRIL DUNFORD:** This one matters more than people think. The category you claim determines what buyers compare you to and what expectations they bring. If you say "online course," they expect $30/month and passive video. If you say "coaching," they expect $500/hour and 1:1 attention. If you say "learning platform," they think LinkedIn Learning. What are you?

**JASON FRIED:** You're none of those things. And I'd argue you shouldn't claim an existing category. You should name a new one. Basecamp didn't call itself "project management software" — we called it "a better way to work together." The category IS the positioning.

**APRIL DUNFORD:** I usually advise against creating a new category. It's expensive and confusing. But there are cases where no existing category fits and forcing yourself into one creates the wrong expectations. This might be one of those cases.

**ANDREW CHEN:** The closest existing category is "professional development platform" but that sounds like corporate HR software. "AI learning platform" is closer but it puts you in the Coursera/Udemy bucket. What if the category is defined by the outcome, not the mechanism? Something like "AI fluency engine" or "professional AI training."

**MICHAEL SEIBEL:** I'm going to be blunt. Most users don't care about categories. They care about "does this solve my problem?" The homepage doesn't need to name a category. It needs to name the problem and show the solution. Let the analysts categorize you later.

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Michael's right for the homepage. We don't need to declare a category in the hero. But we need to know it internally because it shapes the entire framing. Here's what I'd propose:

**The internal category is "AI skill development."** Not learning. Not courses. Skill development — which implies practice, progression, and outcomes. It's active, not passive.

**The homepage framing avoids category entirely.** Instead, it uses what I call "the setup" from my sales pitch framework: lead with the market insight, show the alternatives are broken, define the perfect world, then reveal SkillGap as the answer. The category is implied by the story, not declared in a label.

## Round 5: The Homepage Story — April's Framework Applied

**APRIL DUNFORD:** OK. Now we design the homepage. I'm going to map this to my sales pitch structure, because a homepage IS a sales pitch — just a self-serve one. The visitor is the prospect. The page is the pitch. The CTA is the ask.

Here's how the eight steps translate:

### Step 1: Insight (The Hero)

The hero section delivers your market insight — the one truth that reframes how the visitor thinks about their problem. It's not a product description. It's a perspective.

**JASON FRIED:** The insight for SkillGap is: "You've been trying to learn AI the wrong way." Everyone's watching videos, reading articles, following tutorials. And nothing sticks. Because watching isn't learning. Doing is learning. That's the insight.

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Close, but I'd go sharper. The insight isn't about learning methods. It's about the gap itself. Let me try:

"AI is moving faster than you are. Not because you're not trying — because everything you've tried was designed for a world that moves slower."

That names the feeling (I'm falling behind), validates it (it's not your fault), and reframes the problem (the tools are broken, not you).

**ANDREW CHEN:** I like that framing but it's too long for a hero headline. The headline needs to be 5-8 words. The subhead carries the explanation.

**MICHAEL SEIBEL:** What about just: "Close the gap." That's the tagline. It works on four levels, as the strategy doc explains. Use it.

**JASON FRIED:** "Close the gap" is good but it's ambiguous out of context. On a homepage where nobody knows who you are yet, you need slightly more. What about:

"The AI skills gap is real. Close it."

Seven words. Names the problem. Issues the call to action. Uses the brand line.

**APRIL DUNFORD:** I like Jason's direction. But I want the hero to do three things in three seconds: (1) Name who this is for, (2) Name the problem, (3) Hint at the solution. Let me propose:

**Headline:** "AI fluency for working professionals"
**Subhead:** "The gap between watching tutorials and actually being fluent is where careers stall. SkillGap closes it — with practice, not passive content."

**PARKER CONRAD:** April, I respect the clarity, but "AI fluency for working professionals" is a description, not an insight. It doesn't make me feel anything. The strategy doc talks about anxiety, urgency, the feeling of watching your industry change under your feet. The headline should trigger that feeling.

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Fair pushback. Let me try again. The best heroes do what Jason said — take a stance. Here's my revised take:

**Headline:** "Stop watching. Start building."
**Subhead:** "You don't need another tutorial. You need to sit down with a real tool, build something real, and prove you can do it. SkillGap is where working professionals build AI fluency — 40 focused minutes at a time."

**JASON FRIED:** NOW we're talking. "Stop watching. Start building." is a direct challenge. It's opinionated. It implies that what you've been doing isn't working. And it offers the alternative in two words.

**MICHAEL SEIBEL:** Simple enough to be a bumper sticker. I'm in.

**ANDREW CHEN:** My only concern is SEO. "Stop watching. Start building" doesn't contain the keywords people search for — "AI skills," "learn AI," "AI training." The subhead needs to carry the SEO weight.

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Agreed. The subhead does the SEO work. The headline does the emotional work. Let's move on.

### Step 2: Alternatives (The Problem Section)

**APRIL DUNFORD:** After the hero, we show the visitor we understand their world. This is where we survey the alternatives — not to trash competitors, but to demonstrate that we understand WHY everything they've tried hasn't worked. This builds trust. If we can describe their experience better than they can, they'll believe we can solve it.

**JASON FRIED:** This is where you can be ruthless. Not mean — honest. Name the alternatives and their failure modes:

- "You watched the tutorial. You still can't do the thing."
- "You bought the course. It was outdated before you finished it."
- "You saved 47 bookmarks. You've opened three."

This is the section that makes people nod and think "this company gets me."

**PARKER CONRAD:** I'd frame it as the fragmentation problem. Right now, if you want to build AI fluency, you need: YouTube for content, a course for structure, a Discord for community, LinkedIn for credentials, and your own discipline for accountability. Five tools. No integration. No proof it's working. SkillGap is the compound alternative.

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Both of those are good. Jason's version is emotional and personal. Parker's version is structural and logical. The homepage needs both. Lead with Jason's emotional recognition, then pivot to Parker's structural diagnosis.

Here's how I'd structure this section:

**Section heading:** "Sound familiar?"

Three cards or blocks:
1. "You watched the tutorial. You still can't do the thing." — Because watching isn't doing. Most learning platforms stop at the video. SkillGap starts where they stop.
2. "The course you bought is already outdated." — AI moves weekly. Courses move yearly. By the time it's published, it's behind.
3. "You're learning alone and can't tell if you're on track." — No feedback. No community. No proof. Just you, a browser tab, and growing anxiety.

Then a pivot line: "It's not a you problem. It's a tools problem."

### Step 3: Perfect World (The Vision)

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Now we paint the picture of what the ideal solution would look like — without naming SkillGap yet. This gets the visitor nodding along to the criteria that happen to perfectly describe our product.

**ANDREW CHEN:** "Imagine if you could sit down for 40 minutes, learn something current and practical, build something real with the actual tool, get feedback on what you built, and walk away with proof you can do it. Now imagine doing that alongside other professionals who are on the same journey."

**APRIL DUNFORD:** That's the perfect world. And notice — it maps exactly to our four value clusters: DO the thing (practice), current (freshness), proof (skill graph), not alone (rooms). The visitor reads this and thinks "yes, that's exactly what I want." Then we reveal that it exists.

### Step 4: Introduction (The Product Reveal)

**APRIL DUNFORD:** This is where SkillGap enters the story. Not as a list of features — as the answer to the perfect world we just described.

**MICHAEL SEIBEL:** Keep it short. "That's SkillGap. One sentence." Don't over-explain. Show the product.

**PARKER CONRAD:** I want a product screenshot here. Not a marketing illustration — the actual product. The session view from the v2 prototype. A person mid-path, with the mission card, the room sidebar showing other people, the progress bar. One image that shows the compound system in action.

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Agreed. The product reveal should be 80% visual, 20% text. One sentence: "SkillGap is where working professionals build AI fluency — with practice, not passive content." Then the screenshot. Let the product speak.

**JASON FRIED:** And make the screenshot feel real. Not a polished mockup. Show a real path name, a real mission, real people in the room. Authenticity over polish.

### Step 5: Differentiated Value (The Three Pillars)

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Now we unpack the four value clusters. This is the meat of the page. Each cluster gets its own section with a clear headline, a brief explanation, and a visual.

**APRIL DUNFORD:** But here's my strong opinion: don't do four. Do three. Four is one too many to remember. Group them. Here's what I'd do:

**Pillar 1: "Learn by doing, not watching."**
Task-first paths. Real tools. Real missions. AI feedback. You build something every session. This is the primary differentiator — the thing that most directly addresses the #1 failure mode of alternatives (passive consumption).

**Pillar 2: "Never outdated. Never alone."**
Freshness (intelligence engine stays current) + community (rooms, sprints, other professionals). These are paired because they address the two biggest emotional pain points: "everything is already stale" and "I'm doing this by myself." Together they create the feeling of being part of something alive and current.

**Pillar 3: "Proof you can do it."**
Skill graph. Verified credentials. Portfolio of demonstrated competency. This is the outcome — the thing you take away from all the learning and practicing. It answers "what do I get out of this?" in a concrete, shareable way.

**PARKER CONRAD:** I notice you collapsed the compound system message. Where does that live?

**APRIL DUNFORD:** It doesn't get its own section. The compound value is implicit in HOW the three pillars connect. After the three pillars, one sentence: "These aren't separate products. They're one system — your learning paths feed your skill graph, rooms keep you accountable, and everything compounds over time." That's the compound message. It's a connective sentence, not a section.

**JASON FRIED:** I love that. Don't make "compound system" a selling point. Make it an "oh, that's clever" realization.

### Step 6: Proof (Social Proof + Traction)

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Every claim needs evidence. For a pre-launch or early-stage product, this looks different than for an established one. What do we have?

**MICHAEL SEIBEL:** At launch? Nothing. And that's fine. Don't fake it. Don't put up fake testimonials or inflated numbers. Show what you can: "Join 47 professionals building AI fluency." A real number, even if it's small, is more trustworthy than a fake big one.

**ANDREW CHEN:** The live room activity IS proof. "Right now: 23 professionals are in a sprint." That's real-time social proof that's more powerful than a static testimonial. It says "this is happening, not just existing."

**JASON FRIED:** You know what I'd put here? The skill graph demo. Show a real (anonymized) skill graph. "This is what 4 weeks of SkillGap looks like." That's proof of the outcome. Not a testimonial — a result.

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Here's what I'd do at each stage:

**Pre-launch:** "Join the founding cohort" + Spike event testimonials (you have these from Phase 0 events).

**Month 1-3:** Real counter ("47 professionals building fluency") + one or two genuine quotes from founding members + a skill graph example.

**Month 6+:** Counter + credential examples + "professionals from [company logos]" + specific outcome stats ("Average path completion: 73%. Average time to first credential: 8 days.").

### Step 7: Objections (FAQ or Anticipation)

**APRIL DUNFORD:** What are the top 3 objections that stop someone from signing up?

**MICHAEL SEIBEL:** "I don't have time." "Is this just another course I won't finish?" "Is it worth $129/month?"

**JASON FRIED:** Address them directly on the page, not in a buried FAQ.

"**30–60 minutes.** Not a course. Not a bootcamp. One path. One sitting. Done."

"**You'll actually finish.** 73% completion rate because you're doing, not watching. And the room full of people keeps you honest."

"**Less than a Maven course.** One Maven course = $500 for one topic. SkillGap = unlimited topics, ongoing, compounding."

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Weave these into the page as you go — not in a separate section. The "30-60 minutes" point belongs near the hero. The completion rate belongs near the proof section. The price comparison belongs near the CTA.

### Step 8: The Ask (The CTA)

**APRIL DUNFORD:** The CTA must be specific, low-friction, and repeated.

**ANDREW CHEN:** "Start your 14-day free trial." No credit card required. Appears at least three times on the page: hero, mid-page, and final section.

**MICHAEL SEIBEL:** One CTA. Not "start free trial" AND "book a demo" AND "join waitlist." One action. One button. Everywhere.

**JASON FRIED:** And the final CTA section should be emotional, not transactional. Don't end with a form. End with a feeling.

"The gap is growing every week. The best time to start was last month. The second best time is now."

Then the button.

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Agreed. The final CTA is the crescendo. It brings back the core tension (the gap is real) and resolves it (you can close it, starting now). One button. High contrast. Impossible to miss.

## Round 6: Page Structure — April's Final Decision

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Let me lay out the final page architecture. This follows the sales pitch framework exactly:

### Section 1: Hero (Insight + The Ask)
- **Headline:** "Stop watching. Start building."
- **Subhead:** "You don't need another tutorial. You need to sit down with a real AI tool, build something real, and prove you can do it. SkillGap is where working professionals build AI fluency — 40 focused minutes at a time."
- **CTA:** "Start your 14-day free trial" (no credit card required)
- **Visual:** Subtle product hint — the live room counter, or a small credential card, something that signals "this is a real product with real activity"

### Section 2: Problem Recognition (Alternatives)
- **Heading:** "Sound familiar?"
- Three honest, punchy recognitions of what the visitor has already tried
- Each one names an alternative and its failure mode
- **Pivot line:** "It's not a you problem. It's a tools problem."

### Section 3: Product Reveal (Introduction)
- **One sentence:** "SkillGap is one system that makes AI fluency automatic."
- **Visual:** Full product screenshot — a real session in progress. Path content on the left, room participants on the right, progress bar, mission card. The compound system shown, not described.
- **Context line beneath screenshot:** "This is a 40-minute learning session. You're on Module 3 of 'Ship Your First Feature with Cursor.' 12 other engineers are sprinting alongside you."

### Section 4: Three Pillars (Differentiated Value)

**Pillar 1: "Learn by doing, not watching."**
Brief copy + visual of a mission card with a real prompt and "Launch in Cursor" button.

**Pillar 2: "Never outdated. Never alone."**
Brief copy + visual of the room with live participants + a "trending this week" content card showing fresh topics.

**Pillar 3: "Proof you can do it."**
Brief copy + visual of a skill graph and a shareable credential card.

**Connecting sentence:** "Not three tools. One system. Your paths feed your skill graph. Rooms keep you accountable. Everything compounds."

### Section 5: How It Works (Perfect World, Operationalized)
- **Heading:** "40 minutes. One complete loop."
- Four steps in a horizontal row:
  1. **Watch** — A focused 5–8 min segment from the best creator on the topic
  2. **Do** — Open the real tool. Complete a structured mission.
  3. **Check** — Submit your work. Get AI feedback against a rubric.
  4. **Reflect** — What did you learn? How does it apply to your work?
- **Below:** "Then close your laptop. You just built a real skill."

### Section 6: Proof
- Live counter: "X professionals building fluency right now"
- Credential example + skill graph example
- Early testimonials or Spike event quotes
- Outcome stats when available

### Section 7: Pricing (Simple)
- **Heading:** "One plan. Everything."
- Price card (from the pricing page). $129/month, $299/quarter, $999/year.
- Feature bullets: unlimited paths, rooms, skill graph, AI feedback, credentials
- Founding rate callout
- CTA button

### Section 8: Final CTA (The Ask)
- Dark section (forest-900)
- **Heading:** "The gap is growing. Start closing it."
- **Subhead:** "14 days free. No credit card. Cancel anytime."
- Big CTA button
- "Join [X] professionals building AI fluency"

## April's Final Verdict

**APRIL DUNFORD:** Here's where I land.

The page structure follows the sales pitch framework because it works. Setup → Follow-through → Ask. It respects the visitor's intelligence by addressing their actual experience before pitching the product.

The hero is "Stop watching. Start building." because it takes a stance. It names the problem. It offers the alternative. It's memorable. And it's true.

The three pillars are (1) learn by doing, (2) never outdated and never alone, (3) proof you can do it. Three, not four, because humans remember three things.

The product screenshot is non-negotiable. Show the real product. If the product isn't ready to be shown, you're not ready for a homepage. Build the product first.

Pricing lives on the homepage because one plan with one price is simple enough to show inline. It becomes a feature of the page, not a separate destination.

I'm taking Parker's A/B test suggestion seriously. Test "Stop watching. Start building." (emotional) against "One system for AI fluency." (structural). Let the data decide which resonates more with the best-fit customer. Both are true. One will convert better.

I'm taking Michael's minimal version seriously too. Build the full page but also build a stripped version. Test both. Your first 100 visitors will tell you more than we can.
