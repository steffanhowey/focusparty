Strategic Frame

The story this page needs to tell

The homepage has one job: convert a skeptical, time-poor professional into a signup. That professional has tried to learn AI before. They opened YouTube, got overwhelmed, and closed the tab. They bought a course and watched 20%. They scrolled threads and felt informed for a day. They are the "closed-tab professional" from our positioning canvas — and they need to believe that this time will be different.

The page structure follows the classic Masterclass/Brilliant pattern: aspirational hook → problem amplification → credible solution → proof it works → clear next step. But our version is grounded in the brand voice: calm, specific, warm, and direct. No hype. No "revolutionize." No exclamation marks doing emotional labor. Every section earns the next scroll.

The emotional arc

Each section moves the visitor through a deliberate emotional sequence. This isn't a feature tour — it's a story with rising action. Community runs through the whole thing like a thread — it's not a section, it's the texture.

Recognition → "That's me." The hero names the feeling they already have — and hints they're not the only one feeling it.
Tension → "It's worse than I thought." The problem gets specific and urgent.
Relief → "There's a way through this." The solution appears — calm, clear, structured.
Desire → "I want that." Real paths for every function, every gap, every level. The skill graph. The growth trajectory.
Belonging → "Other people are doing this right now." The rooms, the live activity, the end of isolation.
Proof → "It works." Social proof, credentials, real outcomes.
Action → "I'm starting now." The signup moment, with the friction removed.

Two principles
Conversion: Every section should be strong enough to convert on its own. CTAs appear at natural decision points. The page never goes more than two sections without a conversion opportunity.

Community as texture: Community doesn't live in one section — it's threaded throughout. The hero has a live counter. Path cards show active learners. The skill graph shows peer benchmarks. The rooms section is the crescendo, not the introduction. By the time someone reaches section 6, they've already absorbed that SkillGap is a place with people in it — the rooms section just makes it explicit and irresistible.

The Problem
First scroll · Making the pain specific
Tension

Job: Articulate the problem better than the visitor can articulate it themselves. Make the invisible gap visible. Raise the stakes just enough that doing nothing feels worse than trying.

Strategic logic: This section is Dunford's "setup" — teaching the customer how to think about the market before pitching the solution. The five competitive alternatives from our positioning canvas all fail in specific ways. This section names those failures without naming the alternatives. The visitor recognizes their own behavior in each one.

Section headline
Direction
You've tried to learn AI.
Here's why it didn't stick.
Copy structure

Three or four failure patterns, each rendered as a short, punchy card. Not paragraphs — visual blocks that the eye can scan in 3 seconds each. The visitor should see their own experience reflected back at them.

Failure pattern 1
You watched the tutorial. All 45 minutes of it. Then you opened the tool and stared at a blank screen. Watching someone do the thing is not the same as doing the thing.
Failure pattern 2
You bought the course. Great first module. Then life happened. You're on lesson 4 of 47 and it's been three months. The course was designed for someone with unlimited free time. That's not you.
Failure pattern 3
You followed the feed. Every AI thread, every launch announcement, every "10 tools you need." You felt informed for a day. Then last week's tools became this week's noise. Staying current by scrolling is a full-time job.
Failure pattern 4
You did nothing and hoped. Told yourself you'd get to it next week. Rationalized that your current skills are fine. Meanwhile the gap between where you are and where your field is heading grew a little wider. Quietly. Every week.

Closing line: Something that transitions from "the problem" to "the solution" without being salesy. Direction: "The problem isn't motivation. It's that nothing you've tried was built for how you actually learn."

Visual direction: Light background shift — move from the dark hero into a cream or light sage. Each failure pattern as a card with a subtle icon or visual marker. The cards should feel like a diagnostic, not an accusation. Generous whitespace. The visual tone says "we understand" not "you failed." Consider subtle animation: each card fading in as you scroll past it (intersection observer, staggered).

Section headline

The learning loop

This is the product's core mechanism, rendered visually as a four-step loop. Each step gets a short label and a one-line description. The loop should feel tight, intentional, and finishable.

Step 1 — Watch
A focused segment from a trusted creator. Not 45 minutes of filler — the 6 minutes that actually matter.
Step 2 — Do
Open the real tool. Follow a structured mission. Build something that didn't exist before you sat down.
Step 3 — Check
Submit what you built. Get AI feedback on whether it actually works — not a quiz, a real evaluation.
Step 4 — Reflect
Connect what you just did to your actual work. Write one sentence about what changed. Move on with your day.

Key message below the loop: "Every path ends with something you built, feedback you can use, and a skill added to your portfolio. Not 'hours watched.' Not 'modules completed.' Proof."

Think Brilliant's interactive problem previews: you can almost feel what it's like to use the product. The visual energy picks up here — this is the "relief" beat, so the design should feel open, clear, and inviting after the tension of section 2.

Every Gap Has a Path

Desire

Strategic logic: Masterclass shows the instructor roster — breadth is the whole point. "120+ instructors." You scroll through and inevitably find someone you admire. The key shift from v1 of this section: it's not "here are some sample paths" — it's "we've mapped every AI skill gap for your profession."

Content structure

Each card should feel alive: the activity count uses a green dot, the freshness timestamp is small but visible. Belonging

Strategic logic: "You're not learning alone" is the fifth messaging pillar — and arguably the one with the most emotional weight. Rooms kill that feeling.

The key insight: the room doesn't need to be explained in detail. It just needs to feel alive. Don't describe the sprint timer mechanics. Don't explain the AI host. Show a room with people in it, mid-sprint, with energy. The visitor should feel like they're looking through a window into a place they want to be.

Structural Notes

Page-level decisions

CTA placement

Primary CTAs appear in four positions: the hero (section 1), the solution section (section 3), the path showcase (section 4), and the final close (section 10). Secondary CTAs appear in sections 5 (skill graph), 6 (rooms), and 9 (credentials). The page never goes more than two sections without a conversion opportunity. Each CTA is worded differently to match the emotional beat: "Start learning free" (hero), "Find your first path" (paths), "Start building your skill graph" (graph), "Join a room" (rooms), the final close returns to "Start learning free" for full-circle closure.

Navigation

Sticky top nav with: Logo (left), "How it works" / "Paths" / "Pricing" (center links), "Log in" (right, text), "Start free" (right, button). The nav should be transparent over the dark hero, then gain a white background with subtle border on scroll. Standard pattern — no need to reinvent.

Mobile considerations

The scroll story works naturally on mobile — single column, top to bottom. The path showcase (section 4) becomes a horizontal swipe carousel. The skill graph mockup scales to a simplified card view. The room mockup simplifies to participant count + timer. Testimonials stack vertically. All animations should be reduced or removed on mobile to preserve performance.

Page length

Ten sections sounds long. It isn't — if each section is focused and scannable. The target is 4-5 viewport heights on desktop. Sections 6 (social proof) and 8 (freshness) are deliberately compact. The page should feel like a smooth, continuous story — not a marathon. If the visitor is scanning, they should be able to get the gist from headlines alone in under 20 seconds.

Loading and performance

The Masterclass-inspired dark hero with ambient motion needs to feel instant. No loading spinners, no layout shift. The hero renders server-side with the animation layered on after hydration. Images below the fold are lazy-loaded. Path cards in section 4 can be fetched client-side if needed, but the first 6 should be in the initial payload. Target: LCP under 2.5s, CLS zero.

The community thread — summary

Community isn't a section. It's a texture that runs through the entire page. Here's where it appears:

Community signals by section
Section 1 (Hero) — Live counter: "2,400 professionals building AI fluency this week." Avatar strip.
Section 4 (Paths) — Activity on each card: "47 practicing this week." Paths feel alive, not static.
Section 5 (Skill Graph) — Peer benchmarks: "Market average for marketers: Proficient." Your graph exists in a community context.
Section 6 (Rooms) — The crescendo. Full room mockup, real-time indicator, session schedule. This is where the thread becomes explicit.
Section 7 (Proof) — Real testimonials and credential cards. The community produces outcomes.
Section 10 (CTA) — "Join [N] professionals." The page ends on belonging.

By the time the visitor reaches Section 6, they've already absorbed — consciously or not — that SkillGap is a place with people in it. The rooms section doesn't introduce community. It delivers the payoff for a promise the page has been making since the first scroll.

What this page does NOT include

No pricing section on the homepage — pricing gets its own page (linked from nav). No detailed feature comparison. No FAQ accordion. No blog feed. No partner logos until we have real ones. No "as seen in" press strip until we have press. Every element that doesn't directly serve the scroll story gets cut. The homepage is a funnel, not a brochure.
